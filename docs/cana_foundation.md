# Cana — Research Foundation

**The theological and empirical model underlying the Cana life-planning instrument**

*(Codename "Cana," after the wedding at Cana in John 2 — the first sign, an ordinary marriage feast made abundant. The product's user-facing name remains "Covenant Life Plan.")*

This document is the canonical reference for every diagnostic question, score, and piece of feedback the application produces. All question generation, analysis logic, and feedback must trace back to a principle stated here. It blends two evidence streams that, encouragingly, converge: historic Christian theology of marriage, and the empirical science of what makes marriages endure.

A note on epistemic status, stated up front and honestly: the empirical claims below are drawn from published, peer-reviewed work and are cited. Where a figure is a widely-reported headline statistic (e.g. Gottman's divorce-prediction accuracy), it is reported as the researchers state it, *with the caveat* that such figures come from specific samples and observational methods and should not be over-generalized. This application is a structured-reflection instrument, not a validated clinical scale. See "Information Limits" at the end — that section is not boilerplate; it is load-bearing.

## Part I — The Baseline: What a Healthy, Biblical Marriage Is

### The theological baseline

Christian theology does not treat marriage primarily as a contract optimized for mutual benefit, but as a **covenant** — a binding, self-giving union that images God's faithfulness to His people. Several threads define the baseline:

- **Covenant over contract.** A contract is conditional ("I will, if you will"); a covenant is unconditional commitment ("I will, regardless"). Genesis 2:24 ("they shall become one flesh"), repeated by Jesus (Matthew 19:5–6) and Paul (Ephesians 5:31), frames marriage as an ontological union, not a revocable arrangement. The practical implication for the app: questions probe *commitment under strain*, not just present satisfaction.

- **Mutual self-giving (Ephesians 5:21–33).** The passage opens with mutual submission ("submitting to one another out of reverence for Christ," v. 21) before addressing husbands and wives. The husband's charge — to love "as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her" — is the more demanding. The baseline is *sacrificial, reciprocal love*, not hierarchy-as-domination. The app measures whether each partner feels known, loved, and supported (reciprocity), not conformance to a role.

- **Covenant faithfulness and forgiveness (Colossians 3:13).** "Bearing with one another and, if one has a complaint against another, forgiving each other; as the Lord has forgiven you, so you also must forgive." Repair and forgiveness are baseline competencies, not optional extras.

- **Christ as the center (Matthew 6:33; Ecclesiastes 4:12).** "A threefold cord is not quickly broken" has been read for centuries as the couple plus God. The baseline assumes a shared spiritual orientation is integral, not peripheral, to the union.

### The empirical baseline

Remarkably, four decades of relationship science describe a healthy marriage in terms that rhyme with the theological account:

- **The positivity ratio.** Gottman's observational research found that stable, satisfied couples maintain roughly **five positive interactions for every negative one during conflict** (and far higher, ~20:1, in everyday non-conflict interaction). Couples heading for dissolution sat near or below ~0.8:1. Gottman names a positive-to-negative ratio of 0.8 or less as the destructive zone, and the corrosive patterns within it the "Four Horsemen."

- **The Four Horsemen.** Criticism (attacking character rather than raising a complaint), contempt (statements from a position of superiority), defensiveness (warding off perceived attack), and stonewalling. Contempt is not only the single strongest predictor of divorce but also predicts physical illness. Gottman's teams reported being able to predict divorce with roughly **90%+ accuracy** from observed interaction patterns — a figure he places around 91% from as little as a few minutes of observation, reported elsewhere as 93.6% accuracy in predicting which couples would divorce. Treat these numbers as striking but sample-specific, not as a guarantee about any individual couple.

- **Repair attempts are decisive.** The presence of conflict does not predict divorce; the *failure to repair* does. 84 percent of newlyweds who were high on the Four Horsemen but repaired effectively were in stable, happy marriages six years later. This is one of the most important and hopeful findings in the field: the skill of de-escalation and reconnection matters more than the absence of conflict.

- **Perpetual problems are normal.** Gottman found that the majority of relationship conflicts — about **69%** — are perpetual and never fully "solved"; the task is managing them with affection and humor, not resolving them. This reframes the app's "tension" outputs: a persistent gap is not a failure, it is the normal texture of a long union.

- **Emotional disengagement** (loss of fondness, the absence of positive affect) predicts a *later* divorce than high-conflict patterns — couples drift apart rather than blow apart. So the baseline includes warmth, fondness, and "turning toward" bids for connection, not merely low conflict.

### Where the two streams converge

The convergence is the foundation's core thesis. **Contempt** is, theologically, a failure of the honor owed to one made in God's image; empirically, it is the top divorce predictor. **Forgiveness and repair** are a gospel imperative; empirically, repair is the decisive protective skill. **Sacrificial "turning toward"** is the practical shape of Ephesians 5 love; empirically, responding to bids for connection is what Gottman calls the basis of the "emotional bank account." The app is built on the premise that the biblical prescription and the empirical description point the same direction.

## Part II — The "Why": Psychological and Spiritual Basis for Each Domain

> The condensed form of each domain's rationale below is surfaced directly inside the application, in the **"About this Chapter"** panel shown at the top of each domain during the assessment. That panel presents the biblical principle (with references), any named books, and the peer-reviewed science, so couples can see *why* each set of questions is asked. This document remains the fuller canonical source.

Each of the app's nine domains exists because *both* a theological tradition and an empirical literature identify it as load-bearing for marital health. The domain weights in the engine (Faith 1.30, Marriage 1.25, Money 1.10, Children 1.10, etc.) are set by the strength and consistency of the empirical predictive evidence, moderated by theological centrality.

### Faith & Calling (weight 1.30 — highest)

**Why we ask.** The single most distinctive and well-replicated finding bridging faith and marriage science is **sanctification** — the degree to which spouses perceive their marriage as having sacred significance. This is not the same as general religiousness (church attendance). It is the internalized belief that the marriage itself is sacred.

The empirical weight here is substantial. In the foundational study of 97 couples, over 42% of the variance in husbands' and wives' marital satisfaction was related to endorsement of the marriage's "Sacred Qualities." Since that 1999 study, over 17 studies have found that greater perceived sanctity of marriage is tied to greater marital quality across cross-sectional, experimental, and longitudinal designs. Greater sanctification was tied to less frequent marital conflict and less use of stonewalling and verbal aggression. Greater sanctification is thought to motivate spouses to invest greater effort to protect their marriage, and predicts less marital conflict, more relationship satisfaction, and stronger commitment. The proposed mechanism: attributing sacred qualities to marriage draws partners' attention toward other positive elements of the relationship and heightens their overall evaluation of it.

Theologically this is unsurprising: if marriage images Christ and the church (Ephesians 5), then perceiving it as sacred is not a pious add-on but an accurate perception that reshapes behavior. **This is why Faith carries the highest weight and why a large faith-gap between partners is flagged as a primary tension** — it is the domain with the strongest combined theological and empirical claim, and divergence here tends to radiate into every other domain.

### Marriage & Covenant (weight 1.25)

**Why we ask.** This domain operationalizes the Gottman findings directly. Questions probe: feeling known and loved (fondness/admiration system), open communication without destructive conflict (absence of the Four Horsemen), reliable repair patterns (the decisive protective skill), daily appreciation (the positivity ratio), and whether the marriage is *growing* rather than merely stable (guarding against emotional disengagement). The covenant frame adds the dimension of commitment-under-strain that pure satisfaction measures miss.

### Children & Family (weight 1.10)

**Why we ask.** The transition to parenthood is one of the most documented stressors on marital satisfaction (Cowan & Cowan). Disagreement on parenting philosophy is a recurring driver of conflict. Theologically, children are framed as a heritage and a discipleship responsibility (Psalm 127:3; Deuteronomy 6:6–7). Encouragingly, research on the sanctification of marriage across the transition to parenthood suggests perceived sanctity could motivate first-time parents to inhibit negativity and engage positive conflict-management strategies despite new-parent stressors. Questions probe shared vision, alignment on discipline/schooling, and whether parenting is crowding out the marriage itself.

### Money & Stewardship (weight 1.10)

**Why we ask.** Financial conflict is among the most robust divorce predictors in the family-studies literature (Dew, Britt & Huston; Stanley et al.). Theologically, money is a stewardship rather than an ownership question (1 Timothy 6:17–19; Matthew 6:24). Questions probe alignment on priorities, recurring financial conflict (reverse-scored), shared values about money's purpose, and generosity — because the biblical frame treats generosity as a spiritual-health indicator, not merely a budget line.

### Place & Home (weight 1.05)

**Why we ask.** Drawn from acculturation and expatriate-adjustment literature: unresolved geographic identity is a chronic, low-grade stressor that drains decision-making energy from growth domains. Theologically, place and dwelling are not spiritually neutral (Acts 17:26). Weighted only slightly above baseline because the evidence base is thinner than for the domains above.

### Vocation & Work (weight 1.00 — baseline)

**Why we ask.** Work as calling (vocatio) is a core Reformation insight (Colossians 3:23 — work "as for the Lord"). Empirically, work-life balance and a sense of meaningful contribution affect individual well-being, which spills into the marriage. Baseline weight because its effect on marital outcomes is more mediated than direct.

### Community & Legacy (weight 0.95)

**Why we ask.** Social embeddedness — being known by a community — is protective for marriages (couples with supportive social networks fare better). Theologically, the "one another" commands (Hebrews 10:24–25) make isolation a spiritual risk. Slightly below baseline as a couple-systemic factor.

### Body & Health (weight 0.90)

**Why we ask.** Physical health, stress regulation, and rest affect a person's capacity to "turn toward" a partner; Gottman documents the physiological dimension of conflict (flooding, elevated heart rate). Couples entrenched in Four Horsemen patterns experience elevated heart rates, increased cortisol, and heightened fight-or-flight responses that undermine communication. Theologically, the body is a temple of the Spirit (1 Corinthians 6:19) and Sabbath rest is a command, not a luxury. Below baseline because it is an individual rather than dyadic variable.

### Creative Life & Craft (weight 0.85 — lowest)

**Why we ask.** Individual flourishing and the use of one's gifts (Exodus 35:31) support personal well-being and prevent the self-erasure that can corrode a marriage over decades. Lowest weight because it is the most individual and least couple-systemic domain — but included because a life well-lived is more than the absence of marital conflict.

## Part III — The "What": What Specific Answers Reveal

The engine converts answers into four kinds of signal. Understanding what each reveals is essential for any feedback the AI generates.

### 1. Reverse-scored items reveal hidden negatives

Items measuring problems ("Money is a source of recurring conflict," "Another place pulls me unresolved," "How much do unresolved tensions concern you?") are **reverse-scored** (`10 − raw`) so that, after transformation, every score points the same direction (higher = healthier). A high raw score on a reverse-scored item is a red flag even when surrounding scores look healthy. The AI must never read a reverse-scored raw number as if it were a positive indicator.

### 2. Per-partner gaps reveal misalignment — but not automatically conflict

The most diagnostically rich signal is the **gap** between partners on the same question. Gap bands (adapted from effect-size conventions scaled to a 10-point metric): ≥5 Critical, ≥4 High, ≥3 Moderate, ≥2 Notable, <2 Aligned.

Crucially: **a gap is an invitation to a conversation, not a verdict.** Two partners can legitimately differ on, say, the priority of creative life. What a large gap reveals is that the partners are *experiencing the same domain differently* — and Gottman's work shows perceptual discrepancy is associated with distress when it goes unspoken. The app surfaces gaps so they can be named; it does not label them pathological.

### 3. The quadrant reveals the dynamic

Each domain falls into one of four quadrants by comparing both partners' normalized scores:
- **Shared Strength** (both high): a buildable asset — plan from here.
- **Shared Growth** (both low): an *agreed* problem — the good news is both partners see it.
- **A-leads / B-leads** (one high, one low): an **asymmetry**. Named directly, asymmetry can be a growth catalyst (the stronger partner helps the other); ignored, it tends to breed resentment. The "Faith asymmetry" pattern — one spouse spiritually engaged, one not — is the most consequential because of the sanctification findings above.

### 4. Pattern flags reveal compound risk

Deterministic rules fire when combinations cross thresholds, because risk is often interactive:
- **Core Instability:** Faith *and* Marriage both below 5.0 — the two mutually-reinforcing domains are simultaneously weak. Highest-priority flag.
- **Active Financial Conflict:** either partner rates financial tension ≥7 — given money's predictive weight, this warrants structured attention.
- **Spiritual Misalignment:** Faith gap ≥3 — the sanctification literature makes this a high-leverage tension.
- **Parenting Vision Divergence:** Children gap ≥2.5 — a documented driver of dissatisfaction in active-parenting years.

### What the trend dashboard reveals over time

Repeated check-ins produce three longitudinal signals. **Mission Drift** (distance from baseline) reveals whether the couple's life is moving *intentionally* or by default — drift is value-neutral and can be healthy growth, but it should be *chosen*. **Value Alignment** (the inverse of the mean inter-partner gap) reveals convergence or divergence — a steadily widening gap is the single most important early-warning signal the dashboard produces, echoing the emotional-disengagement finding. **Per-domain movement** reveals which specific areas are improving or eroding.

## Part IV — Information Limits (load-bearing, not boilerplate)

This is the most important section. The app's value depends on it being honest about what it *cannot* do.

### What a self-report questionnaire structurally cannot reveal

1. **Behavior, as opposed to perception.** Gottman's predictive power came from *observing* couples — physiology, facial affect, real-time interaction in the "Love Lab." A questionnaire captures self-perception, which is filtered through mood, self-deception, and social desirability. The app's "Marriage health" score is a self-reported proxy for the interaction patterns Gottman measured directly; it is not equivalent to them. **The app cannot detect the Four Horsemen in action.** It can only ask whether the person believes they communicate well.

2. **The receiving partner's experience of contempt.** Contempt — the strongest divorce predictor — is often invisible to the person expressing it. A self-report cannot reliably surface it.

3. **Abuse, coercion, or safety risk.** This app is **not** a screening tool for intimate-partner violence, coercive control, or abuse. It must never be used to "assess" whether a marriage with safety concerns is healthy. A high "commitment" or "covenant" score in the presence of fear is a danger sign the instrument cannot see. Any indication of abuse requires immediate referral to professional and protective resources, not a life-planning exercise.

4. **Clinical conditions.** Depression, anxiety, trauma, addiction, and personality disorders profoundly affect marriages and are entirely outside this instrument's competence. The app does not diagnose and must not appear to.

5. **The "two realities" problem.** Because partners answer independently, the app shows *two perceptions* — it cannot adjudicate which is accurate, nor should it try.

### What requires pastoral care or professional counseling

The app is a **prompt for conversation and prayer**, a structured starting point — explicitly *not* a substitute for:
- **Pastoral care** for spiritual struggles, questions of faith, reconciliation, and the spiritual formation the sanctification literature shows matters most.
- **Licensed couples therapy** (e.g. Gottman Method, EFT) when the Four Horsemen are entrenched, when repair attempts consistently fail, or when one partner is considering leaving.
- **Individual therapy** for the clinical conditions above.
- **Immediate professional and protective intervention** where there is any abuse, coercion, or safety concern.

### The honest framing the AI must always preserve

The application surfaces patterns worth discussing; it does not pronounce verdicts on a marriage's health or future. Its numbers are ordinal conversation-starters, not measurements. Its highest and best use is to help two people who love each other notice, name, and pray about things they had not yet put into words — and to know when what they have noticed is beyond what a questionnaire can hold, and belongs in the care of a pastor or a trained counselor.

## Part V — Integrated Theological Sources (and the questions they generated)

The empirical backbone of this instrument is the Gottman and sanctification literature. Its theological depth draws on a set of pastoral works in the Reformed-evangelical tradition. These were reviewed deliberately: where a book identified a marital dynamic not already captured by the questionnaire *and* that dynamic had independent support, it generated a new item. Where a book only reinforced existing themes, it was used for framing, not new questions. One contested area — complementarian gender-role theology — was **deliberately excluded** so the instrument does not score a marriage as healthier or less healthy based on a couple's view of gender roles, a position on which faithful Christians disagree.

**Timothy & Kathy Keller, *The Meaning of Marriage*.** Two contributions were substantial enough to generate questions. First, Keller's central thesis that *the chief enemy of marriage is each spouse's own self-centeredness*, not the partner's flaws — "if two spouses each say, 'I'm going to treat my self-centeredness as the main problem in the marriage,' you have the prospect of a truly great marriage." This converges with Gottman's finding that taking responsibility is among the most effective repair attempts. It generated item **m15** (reverse-scored: tendency to see conflict as mostly the spouse's fault). Second, Keller's frame of marriage as *spiritual friendship* — the spouse as the person helping you become your "future glory-self" — a teleological dimension distinct from both romantic satisfaction and covenant commitment. It generated item **m16** (actively helping each other grow into who God is calling them to become). Keller's covenant-over-feeling theme reinforced existing items without needing a new one.

**Paul David Tripp, *What Did You Expect?*** Tripp's thesis is that marriages erode less through dramatic rupture than through *unexamined expectations* and the slow accumulation of small, unaddressed offenses, met by a daily discipline of confession and forgiveness. Unspoken expectation-mismatch is independently one of the better-supported predictors of dissatisfaction in the secular literature. This generated item **m17** (whether expectations have been clearly communicated rather than assumed). Tripp's confession/forgiveness cycle reinforced the existing repair items.

**John Piper, *This Momentary Marriage*.** Piper's emphasis on marriage's *permanence and eschatological purpose* — marriage as temporary, a parable pointing beyond itself to Christ and the church — deepens the framing of the Faith and Marriage domains. It did not generate a new question; it shapes how covenant-permanence items are interpreted (commitment is grounded in something beyond the couple's own happiness).

**Dave Harvey, *When Sinners Say "I Do".*** Harvey's gospel-grounding of forgiveness and his "the biggest problem in my marriage is me" theme overlap substantially with Keller (self-centeredness) and Tripp (confession/forgiveness). It added theological grounding rather than a distinct measurable construct, so it informs this document but generated no new item.

### The three new questions, stated plainly

- **m15** *(reverse-scored, Frequency):* "When we conflict, I tend to see the problem as mostly my spouse's fault." — High score is a flag. Sources: Keller (self-centeredness); Gottman (responsibility as repair).
- **m16** *(Agreement):* "We actively help each other grow into the people God is calling us to become." — Measures the spiritual-friendship / mutual-sanctification dimension. Source: Keller.
- **m17** *(Agreement):* "I have clearly communicated my real expectations to my spouse — rather than assuming they should already know them." — Measures expectation-explicitness. Source: Tripp.

### What was deliberately left out

Two reviewed works (*Recovering Biblical Manhood and Womanhood* and *The Joy of Christian Manhood and Womanhood*) advance an explicit complementarian theology of distinct gender roles. Because this is genuinely contested within orthodox Christianity and because embedding it would cause the instrument to rate egalitarian marriages as deficient by construction, **it is excluded from scoring, weighting, and questions.** The instrument remains role-neutral: it measures mutual self-giving, reciprocity, feeling known, and shared growth — commitments both complementarian and egalitarian Christians affirm. If a future version wishes to add a complementarian lens, it should be a clearly-labeled optional module, never woven into the core scoring.

### A note on adjustable weights

The application now exposes the domain weights as user-adjustable sliders with a deterministic re-score. This is intentional and consistent with this document's epistemic honesty: the default weights (Faith 1.30, Marriage 1.25, etc.) are a *defensible research-informed heuristic, not a validated psychometric calibration.* Allowing a couple or a pastor to adjust emphasis — and see transparently how it changes the picture — is more honest than presenting one fixed weighting as authoritative. The weights change emphasis, never the underlying answers, and re-scoring touches only the deterministic outputs (overall score, domain health, tensions, goals), never the AI-written statements.
